# Trademark policy

The OpenStudy **source code** is licensed under the MIT License — see [LICENSE](./LICENSE). MIT permits use, modification, and redistribution, including for commercial purposes.

The MIT license covers the code. It does **not** grant any rights to the project's trademarks.

## What is trademarked

- The name **"OpenStudy"**
- The OpenStudy **logo** and **wordmark** (the assets in [`docs/brand/`](./docs/brand/) and their derivatives)

These are claimed by the project maintainer. Trademark registration is in progress (DPMA in Germany first, others later); unregistered-mark protections apply in the meantime.

## Permitted uses

You may, without asking:

- **Self-host** OpenStudy for personal, educational, classroom, team, or internal organisational use, and refer to your instance as "OpenStudy" on its own.
- **Refer to the project by name** in articles, tutorials, talks, screenshots, bug reports, academic work, and comparisons ("I use OpenStudy", "built on top of OpenStudy", "unlike OpenStudy"). Nominative use is fine.
- **Link to the project** or to `openstudy.dev` using the unmodified logo and wordmark, provided the link points to the official project or site.
- **Contribute** forks, patches, and issues that carry the OpenStudy name on GitHub.

## Uses that require permission

The following are **not** granted by the MIT license or by this policy. Written permission is required — email [hello@openstudy.dev](mailto:hello@openstudy.dev):

- Using **"OpenStudy"** or a confusingly similar name as the brand of a paid product, paid service, paid hosting offering, or any commercial good.
- Using the **OpenStudy logo or wordmark** as the primary brand of any commercial product or service.
- Registering **domains**, **social media handles**, **app-store listings**, or **company / product trademarks** that include "OpenStudy" in a way that implies an official affiliation with this project.
- **Modifying** the logo or wordmark and presenting the modified version as an official OpenStudy asset.
- Using the marks in any way likely to **cause confusion** about the source of a product or service, or to suggest sponsorship / endorsement by the project.

## Why this matters

MIT is a permissive code license by design — it lets you build on OpenStudy for any purpose, including paid work. The trademark policy is what keeps the name and logo pointing to a single, identifiable project and hosted service, so users who look up "OpenStudy" find the real thing.

## Questions

If you're not sure whether something falls under permitted use — ask: [hello@openstudy.dev](mailto:hello@openstudy.dev). A short note in advance is much easier than a takedown after the fact.
